<p>In 1900, roughly one in a hundred Christians in the world lived in sub-Saharan Africa. By 2020, the region held 30.7% of the world's Christians, the largest share of any region.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shift reflects a long change in where the world's Christians actually live. On the evidence assembled here, it appears to have been driven more by demographic weight and high retention than by large-scale switching.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-7">[7]</a></sup> The available practice data suggests active observance in many of the countries now anchoring that center of gravity.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

## What this entry asks

- How large is the regional shift, and what can the published data actually say about when sub-Saharan Africa overtook Europe?
- Does the current evidence point more to demography and retention, or to large-scale religious switching, as the main driver?
- Is this mainly nominal identification, or does the available practice data point to deeper religious engagement?

<p>The data shows a completed shift. Sub-Saharan Africa is the demographic center of global Christianity.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup> The pattern points more to demographic weight, high retention, and low defection than to conversion.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-7">[7]</a></sup> The practice data points to active observance in many of the countries now anchoring that center.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>What this entry does not claim is that any of this causes or prevents particular social or development outcomes. That is a separate question requiring different data. Nor does it project these trends forward. South Africa's exception is a reminder that patterns change. What it shows is where things stand.</p>
