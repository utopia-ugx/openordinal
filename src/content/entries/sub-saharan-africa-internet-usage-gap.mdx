<p>In sub-Saharan Africa, 87% of the population lives within reach of a mobile broadband signal. Only 27% actually use mobile internet.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup> That gap, 60% of the population, roughly 710 million people, sits inside coverage, not outside it.<sup><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The story of internet access in the region used to be about where towers had not yet reached. That is no longer the main story. The largest block of people without internet are not beyond the signal. They are inside it, and still not online.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup> On the data examined here, price is part of the answer, but it is not close to the whole one.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

## What this entry asks

- If most of sub-Saharan Africa already has signal, where is internet use still low, and where is it not?
- How far does the price of mobile data go in explaining the gap?
- What other barriers still fit the picture once coverage is no longer the main constraint?

## Where the gap actually sits

The chart below does not explain the gap. It shows where it is. In the GSMA frame for sub-Saharan Africa, the population splits into three blocks: those using mobile internet (27%), those with coverage but not using it (60%), and those outside coverage entirely (13%).<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup>

  title="The first split is between coverage and use"

The largest block is not the uncovered. It is the covered-but-not-using. Whatever is keeping most people offline, it is not mainly the absence of a signal.

For a closer look, this entry uses a 48-country panel: African countries that have both a 2023 internet-use figure and a 2023 price for 1GB of mobile data.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup> Six countries drop out because one value or the other is missing.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup> Even in that reduced frame, the regional split is hard to miss. North Africa averages 80.3% internet use and $0.82 per 1GB. The sub-Saharan average is 42.5% and $2.89.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup>

## Price is part of the answer. It is not close to the whole one.

Price matters. The most expensive countries in the panel mostly sit in the lower-use part of the distribution, and North Africa's lower average price is part of why that region separates so clearly.<sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup>

But the cheap end of the panel is where a price-only explanation breaks down. Malawi has the cheapest 1GB at $0.38, and only 18.0% of the population uses the internet. Nigeria is next at $0.39, with 39.2%. Ghana is at $0.40, with 69.9%.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup>

Nigeria and Ghana are the cleanest version of that point. Nearly identical 1GB prices. A 30.7-point gap in internet use. The panel does not tell us which barrier matters most. It does tell us that once price is almost held constant, the rest of the gap has to come from somewhere else.<sup><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup>

## What else fits the picture

The panel is good at ruling out simple stories. It is not good at naming one cause. For that, broader sources help, provided they stay in their own frame.

ITU's Africa-wide 2024 figures do not plug directly into the GSMA numbers. What they do show is where the remaining barriers are likely to sit: in urban-rural gaps, gender gaps, and the overall baseline of internet use itself.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup>

  title="The broader barriers still cluster in familiar places"

>

Device cost points in the same direction. In sub-Saharan Africa, an entry-level internet-enabled phone costs 99% of average monthly income for the poorest fifth of the population.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup> ITU's Africa-wide 2GB mobile broadband basket is still 4.2% of GNI per capita, more than double the Broadband Commission's 2% affordability target.<sup><a href="#ref-6">[6]</a></sup> Even where signal is present, the cost of getting online and staying online can still be out of reach.

<p>That is where the evidence lands. In sub-Saharan Africa, infrastructure is no longer the main barrier for most people. The remaining gap sits between coverage and use. Price belongs in that picture, but it does not explain enough on its own. Device cost, income, rural access, gender, literacy, language, and relevance all remain in the frame, and fit the evidence better than any single-variable story.<sup><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>What this entry does not claim is that any one of those barriers is dominant, or that the ordering is the same across countries. Nigeria and Ghana show how different the picture can be at near-identical price points. The data narrows the question. It does not close it.</p>
