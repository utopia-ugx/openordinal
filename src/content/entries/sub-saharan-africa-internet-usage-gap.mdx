<p>In sub-Saharan Africa, 87% of the population lives within reach of a mobile broadband signal. Only 27% actually use mobile internet. That leaves a 60% usage gap, meaning people who live inside coverage but still do not use mobile internet, or roughly 710 million people.<sup><a href="#ref-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The story of internet access in the region used to be about where towers had not yet reached. That is no longer the main story. The largest block of people without internet are not beyond the signal. They are inside it, and still not online. That does not erase the 13% who remain outside coverage. It means the larger internet problem now sits inside the footprint of the network rather than beyond it.<sup><a href="#ref-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

## What this entry asks

- If most of sub-Saharan Africa already has signal, where is internet use still low, and where is it not?
- How far does the price of mobile data go in explaining the gap?
- What other barriers still fit the picture once coverage is no longer the main constraint?

<p>This entry uses three separate lenses. First, the GSMA regional frame shows where the gap sits inside sub-Saharan Africa as a whole. Second, a 48-country matched panel, meaning the countries where both a 2023 internet-use figure and a 2023 1GB price are available, tests how far price goes in explaining cross-country differences. Third, ITU Africa-wide context helps identify which barriers still plausibly sit between coverage and use.<sup><a href="#ref-9">[9]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-10">[10]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

## Where the gap actually sits

The chart below does not explain the gap; it locates it. In the GSMA frame for sub-Saharan Africa, 27% of the population uses mobile internet, 13% remains outside coverage, and 60% sits in the middle: reached by network infrastructure but still offline. That middle block is the point of the chart, because it shows that the region's main internet gap now lies inside coverage rather than beyond it.<sup><a href="#ref-9">[9]</a></sup>

<p>In country terms, the higher-use end sits mainly in North Africa, while much of West, Central, and East Africa still forms a lower-use belt even where coverage is already widespread.<sup><a href="#ref-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

## The panel weakens a one-variable story.

In the matched panel, the most expensive countries mostly sit in the lower-use part of the distribution, while North Africa averages 80.3% internet use and $0.82 per 1GB against 42.5% and $2.89 in sub-Saharan Africa. But the cheap end of the panel is where a price-only explanation starts to fail: Malawi has the cheapest 1GB at $0.38 and only 18.0% internet use; Nigeria is next at $0.39 with 39.2%, while Ghana is at $0.40 with 69.9%.<sup><a href="#ref-10">[10]</a></sup>

Nigeria and Ghana make the point most clearly. Their 1GB prices are nearly identical, yet their internet-use rates are 30.7 points apart. The panel cannot tell us which other barrier matters most, and it does not prove that price never matters. It does show that once price is nearly held constant, a price-only explanation becomes too thin for the remaining gap.<sup><a href="#ref-10">[10]</a></sup>

## What else fits the picture

ITU's Africa-wide 2024 figures do not plug directly into the GSMA usage-gap measure, but they do help locate the barriers that likely still sit between coverage and use: urban-rural gaps, gender gaps, and a still-low baseline of internet use across much of the continent.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup>

Device affordability points in the same direction. In sub-Saharan Africa, an entry-level internet-enabled phone costs 99% of average monthly income for the poorest fifth of the population, while ITU's Africa-wide 2GB mobile broadband basket is still 4.2% of GNI per capita, more than double the Broadband Commission's 2% affordability target. Even where signal is present, the cost of entering and remaining online can still be too high.<sup><a href="#ref-11">[11]</a></sup>

<p>What remains, then, is not one barrier but a narrower set of better-supported ones. On the evidence used here, device affordability, low purchasing power, and persistent rural and gender divides fit the data more directly than the other candidates.<sup><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-11">[11]</a></sup> Literacy, language, and the local relevance of content still remain plausible parts of the story, but this entry does not measure them directly or rank them across countries. It narrows the explanation without closing it.<sup><a href="#ref-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
